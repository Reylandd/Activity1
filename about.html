Republic Act 10630


Juvenile delinquency is the term used to describe the involvement of children or adolescents in illegal or antisocial activities. Juvenile delinquents are also known as children in conflict with the law, or CICL. Some of the possible causes and risk factors of juvenile delinquency include poverty, abuse, trauma, peer pressure, family problems, substance abuse, mental health issues, and lack of education. Some of the possible effects and consequences of juvenile delinquency include criminal records, incarceration, social stigma, low self-esteem, poor academic performance, and reduced employment opportunities.
Republic Act No. 9344 or the Juvenile Justice and Welfare Act of 2006 is a law that establishes a comprehensive juvenile justice and welfare system in the Philippines. It aims to protect the rights and welfare of children in conflict with the law, and to provide them with appropriate intervention and support programs.

Major Amendments


  • The creation of a multi-disciplinary team to operate the Bahay Pag-asa, a 24-hour child-caring institution for children in conflict with the law who are awaiting court disposition or transfer.
  • The clarification of the minimum age of criminal responsibility, which is 15 years old, and the exemption from criminal liability for children below 15 years old and above 15 years old but below 18 years old who acted without discernment.
  • The provision of various disposition measures for children in conflict with the law, such as care, guidance, supervision, counseling, probation, foster care, education, vocational training, and other alternatives to institutional care.
  • The establishment of the Juvenile Justice and Welfare Council under the Department of Social Welfare and Development, which is responsible for the formulation, implementation, monitoring and evaluation of the juvenile justice and welfare system
